How to think about effective hourly rate

Use these quick notes when you want the reasoning behind the number.

What it means

Your effective hourly rate is your adjusted annual earnings divided by your full-year contract hours. It gives you a cleaner way to compare offers than using the headline rate alone.

Why unpaid vacation matters

If you are not billing during time off, those unpaid weeks reduce annual income even though the posted hourly rate stays the same.

Why self-paid benefits matter

Health, dental, disability, or insurance costs come out of your compensation if you fund them yourself.

Compare total compensation

A contract with a lower hourly rate can still be the better offer if it includes paid vacation, benefits, or other support you would otherwise fund yourself.

When a higher rate can be worse

A bigger headline number can hide more unpaid time, weaker benefits, or higher contractor costs, leaving you with less real compensation over the year.

Plain-English formula

Adjusted annual earnings = gross billed income - unpaid vacation impact - benefits cost - unpaid sick time - contractor costs + bonus. Effective hourly rate = adjusted annual earnings / full-year contract hours.

FAQ

Common questions about contractor pay

These answers stay short and practical so you can compare offers with less guesswork.

What is an effective hourly rate?

An effective hourly rate is your adjusted annual earnings divided by your full-year standard contract hours. It helps you compare offers more fairly than looking only at the advertised rate.

How do I compare contractor offers fairly?

Use the same weekly hours and weeks per year for each offer, then compare adjusted annual earnings and effective hourly rate instead of only comparing the posted rate.

Does unpaid vacation reduce hourly rate?

Yes. If time off is unpaid, you have fewer paid weeks in the year, so the contract is worth less than the headline rate suggests.

How do self-paid benefits affect compensation?

If you cover your own benefits, that annual cost comes out of what the contract is really worth.

Is a higher contractor rate always better?

No. A higher rate can still be weaker overall if another offer includes paid vacation, paid benefits, or fewer out-of-pocket contractor costs.

Should contractors compare annual earnings instead of hourly pay?

Yes. Annual earnings make the hidden gaps easier to see, especially when paid time off and benefits differ.

Does this calculator include taxes?

No. It is a pre-tax planning and comparison tool. Taxes, province-specific rules, and incorporation decisions are outside the scope.

Can I use this to compare permanent vs contract work?

Yes, as a rough comparison tool. You can model a permanent role by treating paid vacation and benefits as included, then compare the adjusted annual value against a contractor offer.
